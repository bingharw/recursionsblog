Meeting the Eye

Destined to Fade?

In contrast to its prequel, Majora’s Mask emphasises contingency in fate.

24 May 2020

Header image: Suzanne Treister, Fate Worse Than Death (1991-2)

1. Nintendo, The Legend of Zelda (...) : Majora's Mask (2000), Nintendo 64

You’ve met with a terrible fate, haven’t you?1 This question stops the player in their tracks towards the beginning of Majora’s Mask. Accompanied by the high-pitched laughter of the Mask Salesman, it subsequently reappears with the apocalyptic animation that plays whenever the timer runs out on the Night of the Final Day. What we might call the procedural recurrence of this question—the fact that it is programmed into the game’s "fail state"—seems to prioritise the theme of predetermination. With every “game over”, the player is made to reconsider the limits of their agency.

Yet fate is not a straightforward concept in Majora. For one thing, the procedural recurrence of the Mask Salesman’s dialogue is undermined by the experience of playing the game. After reclaiming the Ocarina of Time, the player is never in a position when they cannot use it to Save and return to the Dawn of the First Day. If they ever encounter the fail state, they do so out of curiosity, allowing the moon to crash into Termina. This subverts the implied alignment of the player’s experience with that of their avatar. We let the Boy meet a terrible fate.

2. Nintendo, Zeruda no Densetsu (...) Mujura No Kamen (2000), Nintendo 64

Further complications arise when comparing Bill Trinen’s English translation to the Japanese text. The original line is 大変な目にあいましたねえ･･･.2 This is literally, you’ve really met an eye, but more commonly, you’ve had a terrible experience. The “eye” in question, 目, me, refers to a spot on a side of a die, the “experience” of chance. Here, any “fate” connotes contingency, rather than predestination. Jim can therefore use similar phrasing to describe the rough experience the Bombers Secret Society of Justice had after admitting the Skull Kid.

Setting aside procedural recurrence, stores four mentions of “fate” in read-only memory, two being separate instances of the “terrible fate” phrase. The remaining two mentions are the Owl wandering whether the Boy has the strength to change the fate of this land and the courage to change the fate of Snowhead. Earlier, the Owl substitutes “destiny” for “fate”, asking if the Boy has the power to change the destiny of this land and speaking of the Southern Swamp as destined to fade. In both cases, the original Japanese is one word: 運命, unmei.

3. Vyacheslav Onyshchenko, 'The concept of “fate” (...) in the Medieval Japanese War Tales: its origins and changes through the 10th-13th centuries’, Journal of International Cultural Studies, 16 (2010): 135-147 [Link - accessed 16 May 2020]

4. Paul Varley, Warriors of Japan (...) : As Portrayed in the War Tales (Honolulu: University of Hawaii Press, 1994), p. 86

Early uses of unmei in Japanese culture connected 運, “luck”, with 天命, tenmei, a “heavenly decree” bestowing power over life to virtuous monarchs.3 Over time, unmei came to refer to more mysterious, inexplicable processes governing all life, processes that may or may not respond to moral acts. As civil wars rage on, characters in The Tale of the Heike (c.1330) invoke unmei to explain the nearly helpless decline of the world.4 Commonly, unmei refers more to a set of circumstances that one must respond to than it does to some preordained path or task that one’s actions must fulfil.

5. Farah Mendlesohn, Rhetorics of Fantasy (...) (Middletown, CT: Wesleyan University press, 2008), p. xix

6. "お前は、この先多くの苦難に会う。それがお前の運命…" (...) English text version: You will encounter many hardships ahead... That is your fate. Nintendo, The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time (1998), Nintendo 64

In Trinen’s translation of Majora, friction emerges between this concept of unmei and convectional understandings of “destiny” in the fantasy genre. As Farah Mendlesohn writes, “portal-quest” narratives are often “destinarianism”: an individual enters a mysterious, fantastical world, gradually learning “enough to change it and to enter into that world’s destiny”.5 In such narratives, destiny offers a “rational explanation of irrational action without destroying the mystery” of the fantasy world. Majora’s “prequel”, Ocarina of Time, overtly reproduces this destinarian rationality. In Ocarina, characters unmei to refer to the Boy’s preordained role as the “Hero of Time”.6 However, the dialogue also uses 宿命, shukumei, which more readily connotes predestination and is entirely absent from Majora.
